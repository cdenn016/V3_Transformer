## Source-of-truth rule

The V3 implementation and report show what is proposed and what the code can currently do. External transformer and decoding literature set the standard for language-model candidate selection.

## Newly-discovered canon

- Vaswani et al. (2017), "Attention Is All You Need," arXiv:1706.03762: transformer decoding is driven by model logits over the vocabulary under an autoregressive factorization.
- Holtzman et al. (2019), "The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration," arXiv:1904.09751: likelihood-trained LMs can produce poor text when likelihood is used directly as the decoding objective, and decoding strategies can strongly change generated text quality for the same model.
- Finlayson et al. (2023), "Closing the Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration," arXiv:2310.01693: truncation methods are standard decoding baselines whose behavior must be controlled before crediting a new candidate scorer.

## Memo

From a transformer-LM perspective, the proposed EFE scorer starts life as a decoding-time policy. That is not a flaw by itself; it is exactly where one would test it safely. The red problem is novelty and identifiability. If candidate continuations are generated by the base model and then scored by a scalar combining log-probability, uncertainty, repetition, or future loss, the method belongs to the family of candidate rerankers unless the active-inference objects are explicit.

The V3 generation path already exposes a conventional decoding surface. `VFEModel.generate()` loops over time, calls `forward(context)`, reads the last-position logits, and selects the next token by greedy decoding, temperature, top-k, top-p, or multinomial sampling at `vfe3/model/model.py:1198-1221`. `forward()` returns logits for inference at `vfe3/model/model.py:790-794`. An EFE layer placed after this call is therefore downstream of the LM logits unless it introduces its own rollout model and preference likelihood.

Holtzman et al. are useful red canon because they show that decoding strategy alone can change generation quality while the language model is held fixed [Holtzman et al. 2019]. That means an EFE scorer cannot claim active-inference efficacy merely by beating greedy or naive likelihood. It must beat strong decoding baselines and rerankers under the same candidate pool, rollout budget, length normalization, and diversity constraints.

The active-inference claim also needs to avoid an equivocation on "policy." In transformer practice, a policy can mean a next-token sampling rule. In active inference, a policy is evaluated through predicted outcomes under a generative model [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. Calling every candidate string a policy is harmless shorthand only after the transition and outcome semantics are fixed.

Direct falsifier from this lens: generate a fixed candidate set, then compare EFE scoring against log-probability reranking, length-normalized beam search, top-p sampling, and a calibrated uncertainty reranker. If the EFE gain vanishes under matched candidates and matched compute, the method is not empirically distinct from decoding engineering.
