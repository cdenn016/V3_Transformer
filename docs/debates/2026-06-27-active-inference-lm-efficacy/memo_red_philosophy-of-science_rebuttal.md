# Red Rebuttal Memo - philosophy-of-science

## Newly-discovered canon

- Lakatos (1978), The Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes. Use: a program may protect a core theory with auxiliary hypotheses, so a test must state which auxiliary repairs count as failure of the current claim.
- Popper (1959), The Logic of Scientific Discovery. Use: a scientific claim needs potential falsifiers; a vocabulary mapping that survives every outcome lacks empirical content.
- Guo et al. (2017) and Kuleshov et al. (2018). Use: "uncertainty" is an empirical property requiring calibration, not a semantic privilege granted by a variable name.

## Expert memo

Blue's opening is more disciplined than a weak defense. It concedes that the Research wiki is context, not authority; it refuses train-time replacement; and it gives falsification conditions. That should be acknowledged.

The philosophical problem is the phrase "theoretically legitimate." If it means "can be made legitimate if the missing active-inference components are implemented and validated," Blue is defending a conditional research plan, not the claim that the present V3-style scorer is already active inference. If it means "legitimate because candidate continuations or agent sets can be named as policies," the claim loses its falsifiable edge. Popper's criterion demands that the claim exclude possible observations [Popper 1959]. Lakatos adds the practical problem: without predeclared auxiliary-failure rules, every failed experiment can be rescued by changing the preference distribution, the rollout horizon, the ambiguity proxy, the policy precision, the candidate generator, or the outcome definition [Lakatos 1978].

Red should press the distinction between a formal analogy and a canonical instantiation. Active-inference policy selection is not a slogan about choosing among options; it is inference under a generative model with future outcomes, preferences, and an ambiguity or epistemic term [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. A fixed finite continuation set can be a policy hypothesis space, but only if the model says what future observations would be expected under each policy and how those observations would update hidden-state beliefs. Otherwise, the proposal is an information-theoretic reranker with active-inference vocabulary.

The rebuttal should concede that the research plan is worth testing, then demand a sharper claim boundary: EFE success requires a risk term over predicted outcomes, an ambiguity term equal to or empirically validated against likelihood entropy, and failure conditions that cannot be repaired by renaming a different output as the outcome.
