# Red Rebuttal - active-inference-lm-efficacy

## Concession

Blue's strongest true point stands: a finite candidate continuation set or finite agent-set menu can be treated as a policy hypothesis space in active inference if each candidate induces a predictive distribution over future outcomes and if the model supplies the likelihood, transitions, preferences, policy prior, and policy precision required by the POMDP template [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. Red also concedes that the claim is more defensible as an opt-in, no-grad inference-time experiment than as a training-loss replacement. The code has a plausible hook: `generate()` is no-grad at `vfe3/model/model.py:1164-1165`, calls `forward(context)` at `vfe3/model/model.py:1201`, and already chooses tokens from logits through greedy, temperature, top-k, top-p, or sampling at `vfe3/model/model.py:1203-1220` [implementation-engineer memo; transformer-ml memo].

## Core attack

The blue defense fails at the step from "can be defined" to "is theoretically legitimate once the components are named." Canonical active inference does not license policy selection by relabeling a finite list of strings or agents. In the Smith-Friston-Whyte template, `A` is the outcome likelihood, `B` is the policy-conditioned transition model, `C` is the preference distribution over outcomes, `D` is the initial-state prior, and `E` is the policy prior [Smith et al. 2022; variational memo]. Expected free energy then scores policies by predicted future outcomes: risk is `KL[q(o | pi) || p(o | C)]`, and ambiguity is the expected entropy of the likelihood `p(o | s)` [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. A candidate continuation is therefore a policy only after the proposal states what future observations are predicted under it and what outcome likelihood generates those observations.

That missing likelihood is visible in the current V3 path. `forward()` encodes beliefs and runs the E-step at `vfe3/model/model.py:660-661` and `vfe3/model/model.py:725-734`, but inference decodes `mu_final` and `sigma_final` to logits at `vfe3/model/model.py:791-792` and returns logits directly at `vfe3/model/model.py:793-794`. The scalar free-energy function exposes `log_likelihood` only as an optional argument at `vfe3/free_energy.py:341`, subtracts it only when non-None at `vfe3/free_energy.py:401-402`, and the Phase 3 search found no production caller passing `log_likelihood=` [implementation-engineer memo]. This is enough for a reranker experiment. It is not yet an active-inference generative model with causal future observations.

Blue's sigma argument has the same gap. `BeliefState` carries `sigma` at `vfe3/belief.py:26`, and decoding consumes `sigma_final` at `vfe3/model/model.py:791-792`; those facts show availability, not decision value [ml-engineer memo]. The active-inference ambiguity term is likelihood entropy, `H[p(o | s)]`, not generic covariance magnitude [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. Modern neural confidence can be poorly calibrated [Guo et al. 2017], and approximate Bayesian uncertainty can require explicit calibration before its intervals track empirical outcomes [Kuleshov et al. 2018]. Until V3 shows that sigma predicts future error, calibration, selective-prediction quality, or information gain, calling sigma an ambiguity or epistemic term is an unearned interpretation.

The empirical part is weaker than Blue suggests for the same reason. Language generation already has information-theoretic candidate-selection methods, including nucleus sampling, locally typical sampling, and sampling adapters [Holtzman et al. 2019; Meister et al. 2022; Meister et al. 2023]. A V3 EFE scorer must beat these under matched candidate pools and matched forward or rollout calls. If its ranking matches length-normalized log probability, typicality, preference-only scoring, or an uncertainty penalty, the active-inference vocabulary has not added independent decision content [transformer-ml memo; ml-engineer memo].

The philosophy-of-science memo supplies the boundary condition. Popper's standard requires the claim to forbid possible observations [Popper 1959], and Lakatos warns that a program can protect itself indefinitely by changing auxiliaries after a failed test [Lakatos 1978]. The current defense risks that failure mode: if any failed EFE experiment can be repaired by changing the preference distribution, the ambiguity proxy, the rollout horizon, the candidate generator, or the outcome definition, then "active-inference LM" has become a moving label rather than a testable claim [philosophy-of-science memo].

## Defense

Red is not denying the research plan. The defensible version is: V3 has enough belief geometry and generation plumbing to justify an opt-in candidate scorer experiment, while the theory remains conditional until the scorer supplies real active-inference structure. The minimum bar is explicit. The proposal needs a candidate policy set, a rollout or transition model, a future-outcome distribution, a live outcome likelihood, a preference distribution, a policy prior or precision, and separately logged risk and ambiguity terms [Friston et al. 2016; Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022].

The first decisive tests should be sigma and ambiguity ablations. Red would require shuffled-sigma, frozen-sigma, scalarized-sigma, risk-only, ambiguity-only, and log-probability-only arms, plus matched top-p, typical-sampling, sampling-adapter, beam or reranking baselines [Guo et al. 2017; Kuleshov et al. 2018; Meister et al. 2022]. The ambiguity proxy should predict realized uncertainty reduction, calibration improvement, selective-prediction gain, route-selection accuracy, or another predeclared outcome. If it does not, the term should be called an uncertainty heuristic rather than active-inference ambiguity.

The agent-set variant needs the same discipline. The current model-channel gamma energy encodes `s_mu` and `s_sigma`, then builds transport with `transport_mode="flat"` at `vfe3/model/model.py:1045-1049` [implementation-engineer memo]. That can support a flat route-selection experiment, but it does not establish a full non-flat active-inference community policy. Red's position is therefore narrower than "do not test this." Test it, but do not count a named finite candidate set as theoretically legitimate active inference until future observations, outcome likelihood, and decision-relevant ambiguity are actually present.
