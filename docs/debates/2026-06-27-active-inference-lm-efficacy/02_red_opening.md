# Red Opening - active-inference-lm-efficacy

## Steelman

The strongest form of the claim is narrow. It does not say that V3 should replace its train-time objective with expected free energy. It says that an opt-in, no-grad inference-time scorer could rank explicit candidate continuations or agent sets, using V3's Gaussian beliefs and uncertainty, while leaving the existing training path intact. Canon supports the general policy-selection motif: active inference scores policies by expected free energy and uses policy posteriors that favor lower expected free energy [Friston et al. 2017, DOI 10.1162/NECO_a_00912; Smith et al. 2022, DOI 10.1016/j.jmp.2021.102632]. V3 also has a plausible hook: `forward()` produces logits from final beliefs at `vfe3/model/model.py:790-794`, and `generate()` already performs inference-time token selection at `vfe3/model/model.py:1198-1221` [implementation-engineer memo].

## Position

Red rejects the claim as stated. An inference-time EFE scorer is not automatically theoretically legitimate merely because it scores candidates with a free-energy-shaped scalar. Canonical active inference requires explicit predicted outcomes, hidden states, policy-conditioned transitions, likelihoods, prior preferences, and policy priors [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022; variational memo]. The current V3 evidence shows a conventional logits-to-decoding surface, not a public active-inference policy model: `forward()` returns logits for inference, and `generate()` selects from those logits using standard decoding rules at `vfe3/model/model.py:1201-1220` [implementation-engineer memo; transformer-ml memo]. Until preferences and transitions are explicit, the proposed scorer is best described as a costly candidate reranker.

## Evidence

First, the theory burden is heavier than the claim admits. The standard EFE form is a risk-plus-ambiguity objective over future predicted outcomes, not an arbitrary uncertainty penalty. It needs a preference distribution `p(o|C)` and a likelihood entropy term `H[p(o|s)]` [Smith et al. 2022; variational memo]. Heins et al. describe practical active-inference agents as POMDP agents rather than generic rerankers [Heins et al. 2022, arXiv:2201.03904]. Sajid et al. show that when prior preferences are removed, EFE reduces to optimal Bayesian design, and under another limiting case it reduces to expected utility [Sajid et al. 2021, arXiv:2110.04074]. That is damaging for the claim: if V3's preference is future CE, likelihood, repetition control, or task reward, each choice defines a different model and a different null baseline [variational memo].

Second, the V3 code gives red a concrete implementation objection. `BeliefState` has the right raw material, namely `mu`, `sigma`, and `phi`, at `vfe3/belief.py:22-28`. But the public inference path encodes tokens, runs `vfe_stack`, decodes, and returns logits at `vfe3/model/model.py:660-661`, `vfe3/model/model.py:725-734`, and `vfe3/model/model.py:790-794` [implementation-engineer memo]. The scalar `free_energy()` function has an optional `log_likelihood` parameter and subtracts it only when provided at `vfe3/free_energy.py:327-342` and `vfe3/free_energy.py:401-402`; an `rg` scan found no `log_likelihood=` production caller under `vfe3` [implementation-engineer memo]. The model-channel gamma path also builds flat transport at `vfe3/model/model.py:1045-1049`, so agent-set policy claims cannot assume a full non-flat covariant rollout [implementation-engineer memo].

Third, the empirical claim must beat decoding baselines and compute controls. Holtzman et al. show that decoding strategy can substantially change generation quality while the underlying LM is fixed [Holtzman et al. 2019, arXiv:1904.09751; transformer-ml memo]. Malekzadeh and Plataniotis identify computational challenges around EFE in active-inference planning [Malekzadeh and Plataniotis 2022, arXiv:2212.07946; ml-engineer memo]. In V3, `generate()` calls a full `forward(context)` on every generated step at `vfe3/model/model.py:1198-1202`; scoring candidate continuations multiplies that by candidate count and rollout horizon unless a separate belief-rollout cache is implemented [ml-engineer memo]. The evidence pack also reports a V3 seed noise floor of roughly 0.6 to 1.1 percent CV and a large already-measured learned-transport effect, so EFE gains must be compute-matched and seed-aware rather than credited by comparison to greedy decoding.

Fourth, the philosophy frame exposes a scope problem. Popper's demarcation rule requires a scientific claim to identify potential falsifiers [Popper 1959; philosophy-of-science memo]. Buckley et al. demand disclosure of the assumption structure in FEP implementations [Buckley et al. 2017, arXiv:1705.09156]. The present claim risks surviving any outcome by moving its auxiliary assumptions: if the scorer fails, defenders can change preferences, horizon, transition model, candidate generator, ambiguity proxy, or metric [philosophy-of-science memo]. That elasticity is why red treats the claim as under-specified rather than established.

## Falsification Conditions

A direct falsifier is this: pre-register an EFE scorer with explicit analogues of A, B, C, D, E, an outcome likelihood, ambiguity term, horizon, candidate generator, and policy precision. On a fixed candidate pool and equal forward-call budget, compare it with length-normalized log-probability reranking, beam search, top-p sampling, and a shallow reranker using the same sigma and entropy features. If the EFE scorer fails to improve the declared metrics beyond bootstrap intervals and the V3 seed noise floor, the "empirically worth testing as active inference" claim fails [ml-engineer memo; transformer-ml memo].

A second falsifier targets the epistemic term. Shuffle `sigma` across candidates or replace it with a calibrated entropy proxy. If performance and rankings do not degrade as predicted, the scorer is not using V3 belief uncertainty as epistemic value [ml-engineer memo].

A third falsifier targets preferences. Change the prior preference C while holding candidates and logits fixed. If the scorer's rankings do not move in the direction predicted by the risk term, the active-inference semantics are not live [variational memo; philosophy-of-science memo].

Red's conclusion is narrow: an inference-time scorer is a reasonable engineering experiment, but the current claim overstates its theoretical status. It becomes active inference only after preferences, transitions, outcomes, and falsifiers are explicit; before that, it is a reranker with active-inference vocabulary.
