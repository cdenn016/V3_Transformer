# Blue Opening - active-inference-lm-efficacy

## Steelman

The strongest red attack is that "active inference LM" can become a vocabulary swap. A continuation is not automatically an action, a language model's logits are not automatically an outcome model, and V3's Research wiki is not external proof that expected free energy belongs in generation. The canon requires policies, predicted outcomes, preferences, and a generative model linking hidden states to observations [Smith et al. 2022]. The code also does not yet expose a clean final-belief rollout object: `forward()` encodes beliefs, runs the E-step, decodes to logits, and returns logits when `targets is None` rather than returning `BeliefState` (`vfe3/model/model.py:660-661`, `vfe3/model/model.py:725-740`, `vfe3/model/model.py:791-794`). A train-time replacement would be weaker still, because V3's scalar free-energy function only subtracts `log_likelihood` when supplied and the evidence pack reports no production caller (`vfe3/free_energy.py:327-402`).

That attack does not defeat the actual claim. The claim is narrower: use an opt-in, no-grad inference-time scorer over explicit candidate continuations or agent sets, while conceding that train-time EFE replacement is premature. In active inference, policies are scored by expected free energy over future outcomes, and policy probabilities have the softmax-over-negative-EFE form [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. An explicit candidate continuation can be a finite policy hypothesis if V3 defines the rollout, outcome distribution, preference distribution, and ambiguity or epistemic proxy. An explicit candidate agent set can be a policy over routes or communities under the same condition. This is a legitimate policy-selection layer; it is not yet a full active-inference training architecture.

## Position

Blue defends the claim as stated. A V3 inference-time EFE scorer is theoretically legitimate because it instantiates a standard active-inference decision pattern over a finite policy set, provided the implementation names the generative-model components and logs the risk and ambiguity terms separately. It is empirically worth testing because V3 carries Gaussian belief uncertainty through `mu`, `sigma`, and `phi` (`vfe3/belief.py:22-30`) and generation already has a no-grad candidate-choice surface (`vfe3/model/model.py:1165-1222`). Those facts give the scorer a plausible information channel beyond raw logits.

Blue also defends the claim's restraint. Replacing the training objective with EFE is premature. Friston et al. separate state inference, policy selection, and parameter learning across timescales [Friston et al. 2016]. Transformer LM practice has strong external baselines in autoregressive next-token modeling [Radford et al. 2019], with performance heavily affected by compute, data, and scale [Kaplan et al. 2020; Hoffmann et al. 2022]. V3's current code supplies a generation hook, not a complete differentiable active-inference policy-learning stack.

## Evidence

The variational consultant supports the core theoretical defense: expected free energy is the canonical active-inference policy score, and the finite candidate set can be treated as the policy set only after defining predicted outcomes, preferences, and ambiguity [Friston et al. 2017; Smith et al. 2022]. The transformer-ML consultant supports the restraint: standard transformer LMs are trained through autoregressive sequence modeling, so EFE should begin as a decoding or routing scorer rather than as a replacement objective [Vaswani et al. 2017; Radford et al. 2019]. The implementation-engineer consultant verifies that `generate()` is the practical hook, while `forward()` currently returns logits rather than a reusable belief object (`vfe3/model/model.py:1201-1220`, `vfe3/model/model.py:791-794`). The numerical-analyst consultant supports "worth testing" only under matched compute, fixed candidate pools, seed-aware baselines, and separate logging of risk, ambiguity, and log probability [Kaplan et al. 2020; Hoffmann et al. 2022]. The philosophy-of-science consultant supports the falsification discipline: the claim is scientific only if it rules out observations that would count against it [Popper 1959; Lakatos 1978].

The Research wiki is useful context, not authority. Its Active Inference and Expected Free Energy pages correctly map the program's current V3 model to the perceptual/free-energy side and mark policy selection as an extension. That context agrees with the defense: V3 has enough belief geometry to motivate an inference-time EFE policy layer, while the mapping from active-inference policy selection to language continuation remains a hypothesis. The code facts reinforce the same boundary. `BeliefState` carries `mu`, `sigma`, and `phi`; generation already chooses among next-token actions after a no-grad forward pass; but the public model path does not yet expose final beliefs or a belief-rollout API. The first experiment should therefore be an opt-in scorer layered over explicit candidates, not a training rewrite.

The claim is empirically worth testing for a specific reason: raw LM decoding ranks continuations by token likelihood after temperature and truncation rules, whereas an EFE scorer can penalize candidates predicted to land in high-risk or high-ambiguity future belief states. If V3's covariance is calibrated, this can test whether the model's belief geometry contains useful decision information that ordinary log-probability reranking discards. Current adjacent work on active-inference LLM routing, reasoning-length heuristics, and multi-LLM self-organization motivates the experiment, but it does not establish efficacy for V3.

## Falsification conditions

This claim is not defensible if an implementation cannot specify the active-inference generative-model pieces: candidate policies, transition or rollout model, outcome likelihood, prior preferences, and policy prior or precision. Without those pieces, "EFE" is only a label for reranking.

This claim is not defensible if the EFE score has the same candidate ordering as length-normalized log probability, temperature-adjusted logits, a preference-only score, or an uncertainty-only penalty under matched candidate pools. In that case, the active-inference layer adds no independent decision content.

This claim is not defensible if sigma-sensitive ablations do not matter. If shuffled, frozen, or scalarized `sigma` matches the full scorer on the predeclared endpoint, then V3's Gaussian belief uncertainty is not doing the work the defense assigns to it.

This claim is not defensible if compute matching erases the gain. If ordinary beam search, candidate reranking, or self-consistency with the same number of forward or rollout calls matches the EFE scorer, the result does not support an active-inference advantage.

This claim is not defensible if the ambiguity or epistemic proxy fails to predict realized uncertainty reduction, calibration improvement, selective-prediction quality, route-selection accuracy, or another predeclared outcome. The epistemic term must earn its name operationally.

This claim is not defensible as a train-time replacement until V3 has a live outcome likelihood, a differentiable policy distribution over future trajectories or agent sets, a clean belief-rollout object, and tests showing that training on that objective beats the autoregressive baseline under compute-matched conditions. The present defense stops at the inference-time scorer.
