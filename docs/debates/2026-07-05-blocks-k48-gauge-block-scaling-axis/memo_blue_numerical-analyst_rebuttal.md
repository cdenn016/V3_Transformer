# Memo — debate-expert-numerical-analyst — BLUE — rebuttal — blocks-k48-gauge-block-scaling-axis

## Lens
Numerical analysis — conditioning of the exponent fit versus well-posedness of the effect; finite-precision detection statistics; estimation-vs-detection separation; seed-noise floor.

## Steelman of the opposing position
Fitting `CE = E + A·N^{-alpha}` over a sub-decade x-range is a rank-deficient inverse problem whose exponent is unidentified, so no "scaling axis" in the Kaplan/Hoffmann sense can be certified from these five points.

## My position (in service of BLUE)
I grant RED's section 2 without reservation, and I reproduced it: fitting the offset law to the identical seed-averaged CE gives `alpha = 0.929` vs `n_params` and `alpha = 0.180` vs `n_gen` (a 5.15x swing), with `corr(E, alpha) = +0.976` and `+0.998` — the floor and exponent are not separately estimable — over dynamic ranges of only 0.559 and 0.903 decades; both fits nonetheless return `R^2 = 0.9985` and `0.9966`, so `R^2` cannot even discriminate the two incompatible exponents. The log-log design matrix has condition number `306` vs `n_params` (26 vs `n_gen`), above the Belsley-Kuh-Welsch condition-index threshold of 100 for coefficients "carrying substantial error." There is no reportable exponent and BLUE defends none. But conditioning bounds only the *inverse* map — recovering a slope parameter from the data — and says nothing about the *forward* statistic. The monotone CE/PPL decrease is a well-posed **detection** result that is mathematically independent of the ill-posed **estimation** problem RED refutes: strict ordering replicated across all three seeds gives an exact permutation probability of `5.8e-7`, and the *smallest* per-step drop is 4.1 to 7.0 seed-sigma. RED proved the slope is unrecoverable; that is not evidence the effect is absent.

## Evidence
- **Executed detection statistics (numpy/scipy, seed-averaged PPL `124.57 → 112.41 → 106.20 → 99.77 → 92.15`, per-label seed std ≤ 1.08 from `01_evidence.md:3,40`).** Per-step PPL drops are `12.16, 6.21, 6.43, 7.62`; the smallest is `6.21` (GL6→GL8). Against the seed-mean difference SE `(1.08/√3)·√2 = 0.882` the per-step z-scores are `13.79, 7.04, 7.29, 8.64` (smallest 7.04); against the more conservative single-observation SE `1.08·√2 = 1.527` they are `7.96, 4.07, 4.21, 4.99` (smallest 4.07). Every step clears the noise floor by four sigma or more. Distribution-free: under exchangeability of the five labels within a seed, one seed reproducing the exact strictly-decreasing order has probability `1/5! = 0.00833`; three independent seeds all reproducing it gives `(1/120)^3 = 5.8e-7`. Spearman `rho = -1.000`, Kendall `tau = -1.000`. None of these quantities is a function of any fitted `alpha`, any x-axis choice, or any parametric model.
- **Clauset, Shalizi & Newman 2009, SIAM Review 51(4):661–703 (arXiv:0706.1062), §3+App. A** — least-squares regression on the logarithm "generate[s] significant systematic errors," and "in each case where the estimate is biased, the corresponding error estimate gives no warning of the bias." This is the correct verdict *on the exponent*, which BLUE concedes; it is silent on ordinal detection.
- **Stumpf & Porter 2012, Science 335(6069):665–666** — a credible empirical power law "should span at least two orders of magnitude." The 0.56/0.90-decade ranges here fail that bar for a *law*; they impose no requirement on detecting the *sign* of a monotone effect.
- **Ordered-alternatives (trend) canon — Jonckheere 1954, Biometrika 41:133–145; Terpstra 1952; Page 1963, JASA 58(301):216–230.** The canonical distribution-free trend problem is `H_0: F_1 = F_2 = ⋯ = F_k` against the ordered alternative `H_1: F_1 ≥ F_2 ≥ ⋯ ≥ F_k` with strict inequality somewhere — detecting a monotone ordering without estimating any slope, exactly the well-posed question BLUE answers. The known failure mode of these tests, that they "frequently detect an ordered trend when, in fact, one does not exist" when an a-priori order is assumed, is precisely why BLUE does not feed an assumed order to a one-sided test: the evidence is the *independent replication* of the exact order across three seeds, a permutation statistic that presumes nothing.

## Newly-discovered canon (for 01b_extended_evidence.md)
- **Jonckheere, A. R. (1954), "A distribution-free k-sample test against ordered alternatives," Biometrika 41(1-2):133–145.** URL: https://academic.oup.com/biomet/article-abstract/41/1-2/133/456667 — canonical distribution-free test of `H_0` (identical distributions) against an ordered/monotone alternative; detection of an ordering, no parametric exponent.
- **Page, E. B. (1963), "Ordered Hypotheses for Multiple Treatments: A Significance Test for Linear Ranks," JASA 58(301):216–230.** URL: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/01621459.1963.10500843 — rank statistic L for a monotone relationship across ordered treatments, assuming "data of only ordinal strength"; the seed-replicated ordinal test BLUE invokes.
- **Hansen, P. C. (1998), *Rank-Deficient and Discrete Ill-Posed Problems*, SIAM.** The condition number governs how data perturbations amplify into the *recovered parameters* of an inverse problem; a rank-deficient forward map leaves the solution undetermined while leaving well-posed functionals of the data (here, the ordering) unaffected — the numerical statement of the estimation-vs-detection split.

## Falsification conditions
